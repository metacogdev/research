# Culpability Without Capacity: Liability When Organisational Architecture Forecloses Human Judgment

## Abstract

Doctrines of legal responsibility across intellectual property, public administration, and cybersecurity converge on a common structural premise: that there is a legally meaningful human decision point — a reviewable responsible actor who had the information and authority to have acted otherwise. This paper argues that organisational and technical architectures frequently undermine this premise by separating contextual knowledge from decision-making authority — a condition that AI deployment intensifies but does not create. Across three domains, the paper maps a recurring pattern. Where no human is involved at all, existing doctrine works: courts can identify the absence and respond. Where a human is formally present but structurally unable to exercise the judgment legal responsibility presupposes, doctrine strains: courts reach for transparency, notice, and procedural fairness requirements that address symptoms without diagnosing the structural condition. Where the system produces harm and no available theory can locate a responsible subject with the capacity to have judged, doctrine fails. The paper identifies five recurring fracture points across the three domains and interprets the pattern through Elish's (2019) moral crumple zone framework: the structural position in which a human absorbs legal responsibility for outcomes they were not positioned to control. The paper is diagnostic, not prescriptive. It maps where existing doctrine fractures and proposes four conditions — opacity, authority deficit, capacity deficit, and intelligibility deficit — that mark the threshold between constrained judgment and structural foreclosure.

## Part I. The Structural Problem

### 1. Introduction

A caseworker reviews an algorithmic benefits determination. She has seven minutes per case, a terminal that displays the system's recommendation, and an "approve" button. She does not have access to the model's inputs, its weighting logic, or the data on which it was trained. She can override, in theory. In practice, overrides trigger a supervisor review, an explanation requirement, and a processing delay that her caseload does not permit. She approves. The determination is wrong. The applicant loses housing. The caseworker is the decision-maker of record.

A developer ships a product that includes AI-generated code. He prompted the tool, reviewed the output for functionality, and committed it. He did not — because he often could not reasonably verify ex ante — whether the generated code reproduced protected expression or traced to memorised source material in the model's training corpus. The code infringes. He is the publisher. The model provider's terms of service assign output responsibility to the user. If liability attaches, it will attach to him.

A chief information security officer attests to her organisation's security posture for a regulatory filing. The security stack includes AI-assisted threat detection, automated log analysis, and vendor-managed endpoint protection. She oversees the programme. She does not — because no individual can — independently verify the operation of every automated layer in a system processing millions of events per day. A breach occurs. The detection tool missed it. She is the officer of record — the person regulators, boards, and plaintiffs will look to first.

These are not edge cases. They are increasingly common conditions in organisations that have deployed automated and AI-assisted systems into workflows where humans retain formal authority but lack effective control. In each case, liability doctrine demands an account of human judgment — who decided, who should have known, who failed to exercise reasonable care. And in each case, the organisational architecture had already foreclosed the judgment that liability requires.

#### The structural claim

This paper argues that the central problem is not that AI is new and the law has not caught up. The problem is older and more structural. Doctrines of legal responsibility — negligence, due process, copyright authorship, securities fraud, professional malpractice — differ in subject matter but converge on a common structural premise: that there is a legally meaningful human decision point, a reviewable responsible actor who had the information and authority to have acted differently. That assumption was built for a world in which the person with formal authority also had effective control over the decision. Organisational design has been eroding that correspondence for decades, through the same mechanisms of deskilling, authority separation, and protocolisation documented in a companion paper on the institutional diagnosis.[^1] AI deployment does not create the gap between formal authority and effective control. It makes the gap undeniable, by automating the judgment-shaped work that once masked it.

[^1]: The institutional and organisational dimensions of this condition are examined in detail in a companion paper: Davidson, "The Architecture of Absence: How AI Reveals the Structural Subordination of Judgment" (2026).

The consequences for liability are specific and traceable. When a human is formally present in a decision chain but structurally prevented from exercising independent judgment, the legal system faces a problem it was not designed for. It can detect the total absence of human judgment — no one reviewed, no one signed, no one was in the loop. It cannot evaluate the quality or structural possibility of human judgment where a human is formally present. It can see that no one decided. It cannot see that someone was there but could not have decided differently.

#### What the paper does and does not claim

Two distinctions are important. First, showing that the law lacks a test for meaningful human judgment is not the same as showing that the nominal decision-maker is not culpable. Those are related claims but they are not identical. A caseworker who rubber-stamps an algorithmic determination may or may not bear moral responsibility depending on facts this paper does not resolve — including whether she knew the system was unreliable, whether she had alternatives, and what norms governed her role. This paper's claim is doctrinal, not moral: existing liability frameworks do not ask the structural question, and therefore cannot distinguish the rubber-stamp from genuine review.

Second, this paper does not propose a new liability regime, resolve the machine personhood question, or argue for or against AI regulation. It does not argue that AI systems should have legal personality, that existing liability frameworks are beyond repair, or that the answer is more human oversight. It is diagnostic. It maps the specific points at which existing legal doctrine fractures when applied to decisions the nominal decision-maker was structurally prevented from making.

The paper also does not argue that every instance of AI-assisted decision-making produces a liability gap. Where the human retains genuine interpretive authority, contextual knowledge, and the practical capacity to override — some clinical settings, some judicial contexts, some professional environments — existing doctrine may function as intended. The diagnosis applies to the increasingly common condition in which formal authority persists while effective control has migrated to automated systems, organisational process, or upstream design choices.

#### The limiting principle

A skeptical reader will ask: when does constrained judgment become structurally foreclosed judgment, rather than ordinary bounded institutional discretion? All institutional roles constrain judgment. Rules, procedures, time pressure, hierarchy, and resource scarcity are not new. The paper does not claim that constraint itself is the problem.

The claim is narrower. Judgment is structurally foreclosed when the organisational and technical architecture removes, in combination, the conditions necessary for the human to have decided otherwise in any meaningful sense — when the human lacks access to the information the decision depends on (opacity), lacks the authority to override the system's output without disproportionate procedural cost (authority deficit), lacks the time or cognitive resources to evaluate the system's reasoning (capacity deficit), or lacks the ability to verify the system's output against independent criteria (intelligibility deficit). No single factor is sufficient. Structural foreclosure is the condition in which multiple factors combine so that the human's formal role — reviewer, approver, decision-maker of record — becomes practically impossible to exercise as the law assumes it is exercised.

This is a matter of degree, not a bright line. The paper does not claim to resolve where, on the continuum between genuine bounded judgment and structural foreclosure, any particular case falls. It claims that the continuum exists, that a substantial number of the cases and enforcement actions examined here cluster toward the foreclosure end, and that existing doctrine lacks the tools to distinguish where on the continuum a given instance sits.

#### The three-domain structure

The paper examines the liability gap across three domains: creative intellectual property (Section 2), public administration (Section 3), and cybersecurity (Section 4). These domains differ in subject matter, governing doctrine, and institutional context. They are structurally parallel in the way doctrine fails: in each, the law assigns legal consequences by treating a formally designated human as the relevant legal subject while bracketing whether that person had the information, authority, and practical ability to exercise independent judgment. In each domain, the paper traces a three-stage progression:

*Doctrine works.* The law successfully identifies the total absence of human judgment and responds. No human authored the work: no copyright. No human reviewed before deprivation: due process violation. No monitoring was conducted: enterprise liability. The extreme case is the easy case.

*Doctrine strains.* A human is formally present in the decision chain, but the reasoning reveals friction between the doctrinal assumption of human judgment and the organisational reality. Courts reach for transparency requirements, notice obligations, "not the determinative factor" formulations, and procedural safeguards. These address symptoms — opacity, inadequate notice, weak safeguards — without confronting the structural condition: the human reviewer lacked the capacity to exercise the judgment the law assumed.

*Doctrine fails.* No available theory can locate a culpable subject. The system produced harm. A human was present. The human could not meaningfully have acted otherwise. The entity with effective control — the model developer, the system designer, the platform operator — is insulated by contract, the economic loss doctrine, or the absence of applicable product liability. The pattern that emerges across the three domains suggests that legal exposure concentrates on the entity with nominal authority rather than the entity with effective control — a pattern this paper interprets through Elish's (2019) moral crumple zone framework.

Part III (Section 5) identifies five structural fracture points common to all three domains. Section 6 connects the case law to the theoretical frameworks that name the structural problem. Part IV (Sections 7-9) draws implications, addresses limitations, and concludes.

## Part II. Three Domains

### 2. Creative Intellectual Property: Who Authored This?

When AI generates or substantially shapes creative output, three liability questions arise in sequence. Who is the author? Who owns the result? And who is culpable when the output infringes existing rights? The law has answered the first question at its extremes, is visibly struggling with it in the middle, and has not addressed the third at all.

#### 2.1 Doctrine works: No human, no authorship

Where no human claims creative involvement, existing doctrine handles the question cleanly. There is no author. There is no copyright.

*Thaler v. Perlmutter*, 687 F. Supp. 3d 140 (D.D.C. 2023), affirmed No. 23-5233 (D.C. Cir. Mar. 18, 2025), is the leading authority. Stephen Thaler sought copyright registration for "A Recent Entrance to Paradise," a visual work autonomously generated by his "Creativity Machine" AI system, listing the AI as sole author. The D.C. Circuit unanimously affirmed denial, holding that the Copyright Act requires human authorship "in the first instance." The court grounded this in the Act's structure: its provisions on ownership, duration, inheritance, and signature all presuppose a human author. The constitutional question — whether the Copyright Clause's reference to "Authors" is inherently limited to natural persons — was left unaddressed. Critically, the court reserved the harder question: "Those line-drawing disagreements over how much artificial intelligence contributed to a particular human author's work are neither here nor there in this case."

The U.S. Copyright Office had already reached the same conclusion on its own authority. In the *A Recent Entrance to Paradise* Review Board decision (Feb. 14, 2022), the USCO denied registration where the applicant's own characterisation described the work as autonomously machine-generated. The applicant's honesty about the machine's role was legally fatal — a dynamic that creates obvious incentives for less candour in future applications.

The DABUS patent decisions, while doctrinally distinct, trace a parallel jurisdictional consensus that reinforces the broader natural-person attribution baseline. Across the United States (*Thaler v. Vidal*, 43 F.4th 1207 (Fed. Cir. 2022), cert. denied), the United Kingdom (*Thaler v. Comptroller-General* [2023] UKSC 49), the European Patent Office (J 0008/20, ECLI:EP:BA:2021:J000820.20211221), and Australia (*Commissioner of Patents v. Thaler* [2022] FCAFC 62), the answer is the same: natural-person inventorship is required, and ownership of the machine does not supply entitlement. South Africa granted a DABUS patent through its non-substantive examination system, but the grant involved no reasoned adjudication and carries no precedential weight — it is a registration-system artefact, not a doctrinal endorsement.

The negative rule is stable. No machine authorship. No machine inventorship. Ownership of the machine does no doctrinal work. At the extremes, the law knows what it thinks.

#### 2.2 Doctrine strains: The human is present but the authorship threshold is unmapped

The harder cases involve mixed human-AI workflows where the human contributed something — prompts, parameter selections, post-generation edits, curatorial choices — but the AI system determined the expressive elements that would ordinarily ground authorship. The law has begun splitting unified expressive objects into protectable and unprotectable layers, drawing the line case by case without a general standard.

**The split-protection regime.** The USCO's decision in *Zarya of the Dawn* (Feb. 21, 2023) established the template. Kristina Kashtanova's graphic novel combined human-authored text with Midjourney-generated images. The USCO found the text copyrightable, the selection and arrangement of text and images copyrightable as a compilation, but the individual AI-generated images not copyrightable because Midjourney "originated the traditional elements of authorship." The same final page was split into protectable and unprotectable components — a legal separation between elements that are visually inseparable.

**Prompting as insufficient control.** The *Théâtre D'opéra Spatial* decision (USCO Review Board, Sept. 5, 2023) confirmed that extensive iterative prompting does not establish authorship over AI-generated visual output. Jason Allen created his award-winning image through more than 624 Midjourney prompts, then refined it using Photoshop and Gigapixel AI. The Review Board denied registration for the Midjourney-generated components. Human-authored Photoshop edits were potentially protectable, but the application failed because Allen sought registration for the entire work without disclaiming the AI-generated elements. The practical implication: the most labour-intensive form of AI-assisted creation — iterative prompting over hundreds of cycles — does not by itself establish the legal control over expressive elements that authorship requires.

**Source authorship does not guarantee output authorship.** In *SURYAST* (USCO Review Board, Dec. 11, 2023), Ankit Sahni fed his own original photograph into the RAGHAV application with Van Gogh's *The Starry Night* as a style input, adjusting style-transfer variables. The Review Board denied registration. The human's original photograph was undeniably authored, but the protectable delta — the new expressive content in the transformed output — was attributed to the software rather than to Sahni. The implication for the paper's thesis is direct: the human contributed genuine creative work (the source photograph) but the system mediated the transformation in ways that prevented the human's authorship from carrying through to the output.

**The official framework.** The USCO's Registration Guidance, 88 Fed. Reg. 16,190 (Mar. 16, 2023), established the governing standard: case-by-case review with mandatory AI disclosure. Human contribution is recognised where the human "determined sufficient expressive elements" of the output. Prompts alone are generally insufficient. The USCO Part 2 Report on Copyrightability (Jan. 29, 2025) consolidated this position, adding that where a human inputs their own copyrightable work and it remains "perceptible in the output," they retain authorship of that portion. Creative modification of AI-generated material can also generate protectable expression. But the Report did not resolve the hard question: how to measure sufficient human determination when the systems are opaque, stochastic, and generate outputs that the user cannot fully predict or control.

**Where the reasoning shows strain.** Three points of friction are visible in the decided cases and USCO guidance:

First, the USCO denies authorship to the machine but places disclosure, disclaimer, and misstatement risk on the human applicant. These are registration candor duties, not infringement liability. But they illustrate the structural pattern: the human who cannot claim authorship over the AI-generated elements nevertheless bears the legal consequences of misrepresenting their role in the registration.

Second, the doctrine requires splitting unified expressive objects into protectable and unprotectable components, imposing a legal separation between elements that may be perceptually inseparable. This is workable in principle — compilation doctrine has always involved distinguishing protectable selection and arrangement from unprotectable elements. But the AI context creates a new difficulty: the boundary between human-contributed and AI-generated expression may be impossible to identify with confidence, even by the human creator, because the system's operation is opaque.

Third, and most relevant to this paper's central claim, the gap between practical creative labour and legal authorship creates a structural mismatch between what the human did and what the law recognises. The human who spent weeks iterating prompts, curating outputs, and refining results engaged in genuine creative labour. The legal system does not deny this. It denies that the labour constituted sufficient *control over the expressive elements* to amount to authorship — because the system, not the human, determined those elements. The human worked. The system decided. The law recognises only the decision. In the terms proposed in this paper's Introduction, creative IP doctrine strains because the human creator faces opacity (the system's generation process is not inspectable), intelligibility deficit (the boundary between human and AI expression is often indeterminate), and authority deficit (the system, not the human, determines the expressive elements).

#### 2.3 Doctrine fails: Who is liable for infringing AI output?

The downstream liability allocation remains unresolved. Existing copyright doctrine provides a baseline: the party who reproduces or distributes infringing output is a prima facie direct infringer regardless of intent. But beyond that baseline, no court has addressed on the merits how liability should be allocated among model developer, platform operator, and end user — or how the user's structural inability to verify the output's provenance should affect apportionment, secondary liability, or remedies.

**The litigation landscape.** The major AI copyright cases have focused on the training side — whether using copyrighted works to build models constitutes infringement — and on the model providers' liability. The output side — who bears responsibility when a user publishes AI-generated content that turns out to infringe — has produced procedural orders and pleading-stage holdings but no merits decisions.

In *Doe v. GitHub*, No. 4:22-cv-06823 (N.D. Cal.), the Copilot litigation, most claims have been dismissed. The DMCA § 1202(b) claims were dismissed on the ground that Copilot's output was not sufficiently identical to plaintiffs' code; surviving claims are limited to open-source licence violation and breach of contract. The Ninth Circuit accepted interlocutory appeal (No. 24-6136) on whether § 1202(b) requires exact reproduction — an answer that will determine whether near-but-not-identical AI code reproductions trigger statutory liability. No individual developer has been held liable for publishing Copilot-generated code that reproduced protected material.

In *Andersen v. Stability AI*, No. 3:23-cv-00201 (N.D. Cal.), the court allowed training-stage direct infringement claims to survive against Stability AI but observed that it was "unclear" whether application providers are directly liable for their outputs. Trial is set for September 2026 — likely the first AI training case to reach trial with fair use as a central defence.

**The gap in user liability.** No end user has been held liable for publishing AI-generated infringing content. No court has tested the innocent infringement defence (17 U.S.C. § 504(c)(2)) in the context of AI tool opacity — whether a user who could not have known the AI output infringed is entitled to reduced statutory damages. No "reasonable creator" standard of care for users of AI creative tools exists in any jurisdiction. Model providers' terms of service routinely disclaim liability for infringing outputs and assign output responsibility to the user — a contractual allocation of risk that has not been tested against the user's structural inability to verify the output's provenance.

**The structural problem stated plainly.** The law knows who is not an author: the machine. It has a baseline answer for who infringes: the party who reproduces or distributes. But it has no developed framework for allocating liability when an authorless system produces infringing output, the user often cannot reasonably verify ex ante whether the output infringes, and the model provider disclaims responsibility by contract. The human publisher is the path of least doctrinal resistance — not because the human is culpable in any traditional sense, but because the human is the only entity in the chain that existing liability frameworks can reach without doctrinal innovation.

This is the moral crumple zone in the creative IP context. The human absorbs liability not because they had the capacity to judge — to verify the output's provenance, to assess its similarity to works in the training corpus, to make a reasoned determination about infringement risk — but because they are the only available subject for blame.

The creative IP domain assigns authorship and infringement liability by treating a formally designated human as the relevant legal subject. The next domain — public administration — does the same with procedural validity and accountability. The common structural condition is identical: the law attributes legal consequences to a human whose organisational position did not afford the judgment those consequences presuppose.

### 3. Public Administration: Who Decided This?

When an algorithmic system makes or substantially shapes a consequential administrative decision and a human nominally signs off, two questions arise. Has the affected person received the process the law requires? And who is accountable when the decision is wrong? The law answers these questions through doctrines — due process, procedural fairness, administrative review — that were designed for a different institutional architecture: one in which the person with formal authority also had the capacity to exercise independent judgment. When that correspondence breaks down, the doctrine does not break cleanly. It bends.

#### 3.1 Doctrine works: No human at all

When no human participates before deprivation, ordinary due process doctrine invalidates the system. The extreme case remains the easy case.

*Cahoo v. SAS Analytics*, 912 F.3d 887 (6th Cir. 2019), is the leading authority. Michigan's MiDAS automated unemployment fraud detection system operated from October 2013 to August 2015 without meaningful pre-deprivation human review — the system generated fraud determinations, assessed penalties, initiated wage garnishment, and seized tax refunds through an automated process that the Sixth Circuit characterised as lacking adequate human involvement. The system produced a 93% false positive rate, falsely accusing over 40,000 people of fraud with penalties reaching $187,000. The Sixth Circuit denied qualified immunity, finding the right to a pre-deprivation hearing was "clearly established."

But the subsequent proceedings illustrate exactly how the accountability gap operates. In No. 21-2672 (6th Cir. 2023), the court reversed on qualified immunity for individual officials, finding insufficient evidence connecting any specific official to the system's specific defects. Officials designed a system structurally incapable of providing due process, but the court could not identify which officials bore personal responsibility for which design choices — structural harm without locatable individual fault. The system was unconstitutional, but the court could not attach personal damages liability to identified officials on the record before it.

The doctrinal lesson is precise: the total absence of human review before deprivation is a recognised constitutional violation. The law can handle the case where no one was in the loop. What it cannot handle, as the qualified immunity reversal demonstrates, is tracing the constitutional violation back to a responsible individual within the organisation that designed the system.

#### 3.2 Doctrine strains: The human is present but cannot meaningfully review

The harder cases involve humans who formally review algorithmic or system-mediated outputs but cannot explain, validate, or independently assess them. The cases below span welfare administration, public education, criminal sentencing, welfare profiling, and clinical software — different doctrinal contexts united by a common condition: an institutional decision that formally depends on human professional or official judgment while the operative system remains opaque or structurally dominant. Courts reach for due process, procedural fairness, and transparency doctrines — tools designed for a different problem — and apply them to a structural condition they cannot fully address.

**Unexplained algorithmic allocations.** In *K.W. v. Armstrong*, 789 F.3d 962 (9th Cir. 2015), Idaho used an undisclosed formula to calculate Medicaid budgets for adults with developmental disabilities. Budget notices gave no explanation of how cuts were calculated, and the state initially claimed the formula was a "trade secret." The Ninth Circuit affirmed a preliminary injunction requiring "ascertainable standards" and intelligible explanation. The district court subsequently ordered the state to replace the formula with a methodology that met these requirements.

The reasoning shows strain. The Ninth Circuit's holding addressed the procedural dimension — inadequate notice and the absence of ascertainable standards — which only partially captures the structural problem: algorithmic resource allocation without individualised human assessment. The doctrinal vehicle was notice-and-hearing, which is not wrong but is incomplete. The actual condition was an automated system making consequential determinations that no human in the workflow could independently evaluate. The case settled in 2016 before any court reached a merits determination on whether algorithmic budget-setting *itself*, as opposed to inadequate notice *about* it, violates due process.

**Sign-off without capacity to validate.** *Houston Federation of Teachers v. Houston ISD*, 251 F. Supp. 3d 1168 (S.D. Tex. 2017), is the clearest authority on the gap between nominal review and meaningful oversight. Teachers were evaluated and could be terminated using SAS Institute's proprietary EVAAS value-added algorithm, which constituted 50-100% of their evaluation score. SAS classified the methodology as a trade secret, refusing disclosure to either the school district or its teachers. The court found teachers had "no meaningful way to ensure correct calculation of their EVAAS scores" and noted that when errors were discovered, individual correction required rerunning the entire district's analysis.

The case settled with Houston ISD abandoning EVAAS for termination decisions, preventing doctrinal development. But the court's finding is significant for this paper's central claim: the teachers who were held accountable for their scores could not verify, challenge, or independently assess the system that produced them. The administrators who signed off on the evaluations could not either. Formal review existed. Meaningful review — review by someone with the information and capacity to exercise independent judgment about the score's validity — did not.

**Formal discretion treated as sufficient oversight despite acknowledged opacity.** *State v. Loomis*, 881 N.W.2d 749 (Wis. 2016), cert. denied, 137 S. Ct. 2290 (2017), upheld the use of Northpointe's proprietary COMPAS risk assessment tool in sentencing. The Wisconsin Supreme Court required that COMPAS not be "the determinative factor" in sentencing decisions and mandated warnings about the tool's limitations — its opacity, lack of Wisconsin-specific validation, racial disproportionality concerns, and group-level rather than individual predictive capacity.

The court acknowledged that judges cannot inspect the methodology. It then treated formal judicial discretion as sufficient oversight. The tension is internal to the opinion: the court recognises the tool is opaque but concludes that the institutional authority of the judge — the judge's formal right to exercise independent judgment — satisfies due process. Whether judges *actually* exercise independent judgment when presented with a risk score they cannot interrogate, or whether the score functions as a cognitive anchor that formal warnings do not dislodge, is a question the court did not address. Within the sentencing context, the "not the determinative factor" formulation either renders COMPAS redundant (if judges truly exercise independent judgment) or functions as an unenforceable aspiration (if the score anchors the decision in practice). *Loomis* is a sentencing-specific due process decision; its broader significance for this paper is illustrative rather than directly precedential for other domains.

**Opaque risk profiling struck down, but no positive oversight standard established.** In *NJCM v. De Staat der Nederlanden (SyRI)*, ECLI:NL:RBDHA:2020:1878 (Dist. Ct. The Hague, 2020), the Netherlands' System Risk Indication linked multiple government databases to generate welfare fraud risk profiles, deployed in low-income immigrant neighbourhoods. The court held that SyRI violated Article 8 ECHR, finding it "insufficiently transparent and verifiable" with inadequate safeguards. But the court did not establish a positive requirement for human-in-the-loop oversight, did not order disclosure of the risk models, and did not prescribe what sufficient human involvement would look like. The privacy doctrine was used to do accountability work — a doctrinal vehicle mismatch analogous to the notice-and-hearing displacement in *K.W.*

**Where the reasoning shows strain across the cases.** In the decided cases, courts address symptoms — inadequate notice, opacity, weak safeguards, failure of disclosure — rather than the structural condition that produces these symptoms: the human reviewer lacked the capacity to exercise the judgment the law assumed. *K.W.* uses notice-and-hearing doctrine to address an intelligibility problem. *Houston Federation* identifies the absence of meaningful review but settles before the court can develop a standard for what meaningful review would require. *Loomis* treats formal authority as substantive oversight despite acknowledging that the tool is opaque. *SyRI* uses privacy doctrine to do accountability work. The doctrine bends in each case toward the right result but cannot name the structural condition it is responding to. In this paper's terms, the public administration cases exhibit all four foreclosure conditions: opacity (the algorithms are proprietary or unexplained), authority deficit (the reviewer cannot override without disproportionate cost), capacity deficit (caseloads and time pressure preclude independent evaluation), and intelligibility deficit (the system's reasoning is not accessible to the decision-maker of record).

#### 3.3 Doctrine fails: No standard for "meaningful" human oversight

No settled U.S. appellate standard defines what meaningful human oversight of an algorithmic administrative decision requires. No generally operationalised test distinguishes rubber-stamping from genuine participation in the decided case law. No court has adopted automation bias as a formal doctrine sufficient to invalidate ostensible human-in-the-loop review.

**The regulatory gap.** Regulators have converged on the position that rubber-stamping is insufficient. The Article 29 Working Party (now EDPB) stated that a "superficial check by a human (rubber stamping) is not sufficient" to escape GDPR Article 22. The EU AI Act, Article 14, is the first major regulation to explicitly name automation bias as a risk requiring mitigation and to require that human overseers be "able to properly understand the relevant capacities and limitations" of high-risk AI systems. But no enforcement action has applied either standard. No court has operationalised what "properly understand" means in practice, or what evidentiary showing of automation bias would be sufficient to invalidate ostensible human oversight.

**The CJEU broadens scope but not substance.** In the *SCHUFA* scoring decision (Case C-634/21, Dec. 7, 2023), the Court of Justice of the European Union broadened GDPR Article 22 to cover preparatory algorithmic scoring that third parties "draw strongly" on — even where a human nominally makes the final decision. The court held that if a third party "draws strongly" on an automated score, the process is subject to Article 22 regardless of whether the third party performs additional review. This broadens the *scope* of the protection but does not resolve the *substance*: what constitutes sufficient human review to escape Article 22's prohibition remains unspecified.

**Emerging cases.** *Mobley v. Workday*, 740 F. Supp. 3d 796 (N.D. Cal. 2024), allowed disparate impact claims against an AI hiring vendor to proceed. The court warned against "drawing an artificial distinction between software decisionmakers and human decisionmakers" — language that, if adopted more broadly, could dismantle the formal distinction between automated and human-reviewed decisions that currently insulates many AI-assisted workflows from scrutiny. But the case is at the pleading stage and addresses discrimination doctrine, not the structural oversight question.

**The gap stated plainly.** Many legal and regulatory regimes treat human oversight of algorithmic decisions as legally significant — as evidence of due process, as a compliance requirement, as a condition for escaping strict prohibitions on automated decision-making. But no settled doctrine evaluates whether the oversight was structurally possible. It can see that no one reviewed. It cannot see that someone reviewed without the capacity to judge. Until it can, the gap between formal presence and effective participation will continue to be papered over by transparency requirements, notice obligations, and "not the determinative factor" formulations — none of which addresses the structural condition that the person formally responsible for the decision was not in a position to have decided otherwise.

If the public administration cases show doctrine straining to evaluate whether a human reviewer had the capacity to judge, the cybersecurity cases show a different facet of the same problem: doctrine straining to locate individual accountability when harm results from automated systems that no single person meaningfully controls.

### 4. Cybersecurity: Who Should Have Caught This?

When an AI-assisted security system fails to detect a threat, or when a security vendor's automated product causes harm, and a human was nominally responsible for oversight, the liability question shifts from authorship or procedural review to enterprise obligation and individual accountability. But the same structural pattern emerges: the law holds entities and individuals responsible for security outcomes that organisational and technical architectures had already placed beyond effective human control.

#### 4.1 Doctrine works: Enterprise obligation to monitor, configure, and disclose

The law is comfortable imposing organisational duties — having controls, reviewing logs, configuring tools, disclosing breaches. Violation of these duties is actionable even without identifying a single negligent individual. This is the cybersecurity domain's version of the easy case.

*FTC v. Wyndham Worldwide*, 799 F.3d 236 (3d Cir. 2015), is the foundational authority. The Third Circuit affirmed the FTC's Section 5 authority over data security, endorsing "reasonable security" as an evolving enterprise standard. Wyndham established that organisations have a legal obligation to maintain reasonable security practices, assessed against known risks and industry norms, without requiring the FTC to specify in advance exactly what practices are required.

The HHS Office for Civil Rights has reinforced the principle that automated monitoring tools do not satisfy the obligation by themselves. In the *Memorial Healthcare System* resolution (2017), OCR found that having audit logs was insufficient — liability attached for failure to review and act on the logs' outputs. Having the tool is not enough. Someone must meaningfully engage with what the tool produces. The *Montefiore Medical Center* resolution (2023) confirmed the same principle: automated monitoring that no one reviews does not constitute compliance.

The *Blackbaud* multistate attorney general settlement ($49.5 million, Oct. 2023) went further, directly requiring specific logging, intrusion detection capabilities, and third-party compliance assessments. The FTC's enforcement against *Chegg* (2022/2023) treated repeated compromises as evidence that nominal human supervision of security tools was inadequate — the recurrence itself demonstrating that the oversight was not functioning.

The Capital One OCC consent order ($80 million, 2020) is the most instructive authority on the human-tool relationship. Capital One had a web application firewall. Internal employees had warned about its misconfiguration before the breach. The OCC faulted Capital One not for the absence of tools but for failure to conduct adequate risk assessment before cloud migration, for internal audit failures, and for board-level inaction on known deficiencies. The civil class action settled for $190 million without judicial findings on whether reliance on automated tools without human configuration review constituted negligence. The foundational question — whether having a tool satisfies the duty of care when no human meaningfully reviews its configuration — was raised but not decided.

**What works:** A combination of adjudicated law (*Wyndham*) and a thick regulatory enforcement practice (OCR, FTC, multistate AG settlements) establishes that organisations have enterprise-level duties to monitor, configure, review, and disclose. The duty does not depend on identifying a single negligent actor. The evidentiary base here is more enforcement-driven than in IP or public administration — the cybersecurity domain is substantially shaped by consent orders and settlements rather than merits adjudication. But the enterprise-level obligation is functionally established.

#### 4.2 Doctrine strains: When the tool itself fails or the individual is exposed

When the focus shifts from enterprise obligation to individual accountability — or when the security tool itself is the source of harm — existing doctrine shows visible strain. The SolarWinds litigation ecosystem and the CrowdStrike incident illustrate both dimensions.

**The collapse of individual CISO liability.** *SEC v. SolarWinds Corp. & Timothy G. Brown*, No. 1:23-cv-09518 (S.D.N.Y.), was the first federal action to name a chief information security officer individually for enterprise security failures. Judge Engelmayer's July 2024 order rejected the SEC's attempt to extend internal accounting controls to cybersecurity, holding that such controls concern "the integrity of financial transactions, not detecting or preventing cybersecurity deficiencies." Only one claim survived: that SolarWinds' website Security Statement was materially misleading regarding access controls — the widely reported "solarwinds123" password.

The case was dismissed with prejudice in November 2025 by joint stipulation, with no monetary penalty and no admission of wrongdoing. The flagship attempt to impose broad personal CISO liability for enterprise cyber failure collapsed. The reasoning reveals a structural irony: a company's voluntary marketing representations about its security posture created greater legal exposure than its mandatory SEC disclosures. The perverse incentive is clear: say less about security to reduce legal risk.

**Economic loss doctrine and contractual caps limit recourse against the entity with effective control.** The CrowdStrike/Falcon incident (July 19, 2024) — a faulty sensor update that reportedly crashed approximately 8.5 million Windows devices globally — generated extensive litigation but limited doctrinal development. The procedural details of *Delta Air Lines v. CrowdStrike* (Fulton County Superior Court, 2025) and the related securities class action (*Plymouth County Retirement Ass'n v. CrowdStrike*, W.D. Tex., dismissed Jan. 2026) are still being confirmed against primary materials. What is clear from reporting and available orders is that the litigation illustrates a structural pattern: economic loss doctrine and contractual limitations restrict the legal recourse available to customers of automated systems when those systems fail, concentrating exposure on the entity bearing the operational losses rather than the entity that designed and deployed the system.

**AI vendor deception.** *FTC v. Evolv Technologies* (D. Mass., settled Dec. 2024) addresses a different but related problem: AI security vendors marketing capabilities their systems do not possess. Evolv marketed AI-powered security screening as capable of detecting weapons more accurately than metal detectors. The FTC found the system was fundamentally a metal detector — "the only things that Express scanners detect are metallic" — and one of its scanners failed to detect a knife later used in a stabbing. Evolv was banned from making unsubstantiated AI capability claims. The case establishes vendor accountability for misrepresentation but does not address the harder question: what happens when the AI tool is accurately described but the human operator cannot meaningfully oversee its operation?

**Where the reasoning shows strain.** SolarWinds reveals that the legal system cannot effectively reach individual security officers for enterprise failures — the doctrinal tools available (securities fraud, accounting controls) do not fit the problem. The CrowdStrike litigation reveals that economic loss doctrine and contractual caps insulate the entity with effective control (the vendor that pushed the faulty update) while the entity bearing the operational losses (the customer) has minimal legal recourse. In both, the structural pattern repeats: the entity that designed the system is protected; the entity that relied on the system bears the consequences. The cybersecurity domain's version of structural foreclosure is distinctive in emphasis. Capacity deficit and opacity dominate: no individual can verify the operation of layered automated systems processing millions of events. Authority deficit operates through contractual and economic-loss insulation rather than through role design. Intelligibility deficit — central in public administration, where the reviewer must evaluate an algorithm's output — is less prominent here, because the security officer's role is to oversee a programme rather than to assess individual system-level determinations. The foreclosure conditions are present but weighted differently.

#### 4.3 Doctrine fails: Supply chains, AI-specific tool failure, and criminal exposure

The cybersecurity domain's version of doctrinal failure is distinctive. The gap is not in authorship attribution or procedural review but in supply chain accountability, AI tool reliability, and criminal liability for structurally constrained oversight.

**Supply chain liability exists in regulatory policy but not in adjudication.** No reported U.S. merits decision has imposed liability for failure to detect or remediate Log4j, the xz utils backdoor, or similar supply chain vulnerabilities. No major merits decision arising from Log4j has been located, despite the FTC's January 2022 warning that failure to patch known vulnerabilities "implicates" the Federal Trade Commission Act. No legal response to the xz utils backdoor (March 2024) has been identified — the pseudonymous, likely state-sponsored nature of the attack makes traditional accountability impossible, and no framework addresses deliberate sabotage of open-source dependencies.

Executive Order 14028 (May 2021) and EO 14144 (Jan. 2025) established SBOM requirements and NIST SSDF standards for federal procurement, enforceable through federal contracts and the False Claims Act. The DOJ Civil Cyber-Fraud Initiative has recovered over $52 million in FY 2025. But no court has ruled whether failure to maintain an SBOM or comply with SSDF constitutes negligence per se outside federal procurement. The regulatory infrastructure is building. The adjudicative infrastructure is absent.

The EU Cyber Resilience Act (Regulation 2024/2847), fully applicable December 2027, creates the first comprehensive framework making manufacturers liable for cybersecurity deficiencies throughout their supply chains — the legislative solution the U.S. has not adopted.

**No decided case where reliance on an AI security tool was central to the liability analysis.** No court has addressed whether an organisation that relied on an AI-assisted threat detection tool and suffered a breach because the tool failed has a claim against the vendor, or whether the reliance itself satisfies the organisation's duty of care. No implied warranty of fitness claim for an AI cybersecurity tool has been decided in any jurisdiction. The question of what happens when the monitoring system itself is the point of failure — when the tool designed to detect threats misses them — has no decided answer.

**Criminal liability requires active concealment, not structurally constrained oversight.** *United States v. Sullivan*, No. 3:20-cr-00337 (N.D. Cal.), conviction affirmed No. 23-927 (9th Cir. Mar. 13, 2025), is the critical authority and its limits define the gap. Uber's former CISO was convicted of obstruction of FTC proceedings and misprision of felony for concealing a 2016 breach affecting 57 million users — arranging payments to hackers through a bug bounty programme, having them sign NDAs falsely denying data theft, and withholding information from Uber's general counsel and new CEO. The Ninth Circuit affirmed, emphasising that Sullivan — a former federal prosecutor in a computer hacking unit — knew the conduct was criminal.

Sullivan is important and limited. It establishes criminal exposure for active concealment of a known breach. It does not establish criminal liability for a security professional who merely fails to detect or respond to a threat. No criminal theory reaches the CISO who attests to controls they cannot meaningfully oversee, the caseworker who processes determinations they cannot meaningfully review, or the developer who publishes code they cannot meaningfully audit. Existing criminal law requires a mens rea incompatible with the structurally constrained oversight this paper describes. The gap is between what the law punishes (deliberate concealment) and what the organisational architecture produces (structural inability to detect).

**U.S. courts have generally resisted treating software as a product — and this matters.** The doctrine best suited to systems that cause harm independent of individual fault — products liability — remains largely unavailable for the category of systems where individual fault is most structurally difficult to identify. *Winter v. G.P. Putnam's Sons*, 938 F.2d 1033 (9th Cir. 1991), refused to extend products liability to information products. The Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability § 19(a) defines "product" as tangible personal property. *Rodgers v. Christie*, 795 F. App'x 878 (3d Cir. 2020), held that AI risk-assessment software was not a product. *Delta v. CrowdStrike* declined to treat a cybersecurity update as a product.

A counter-trend is emerging in consumer-harm contexts, but it remains at the pleading stage. The EU has solved this legislatively: the Product Liability Directive 2024/2853 explicitly brings software — including standalone software, AI systems, and digital manufacturing files — within strict liability, with burden-of-proof presumptions favouring claimants where proof is "excessively difficult" due to technical complexity. The U.S. has not.

**The gap stated plainly.** The law holds organisations responsible for not monitoring. It does not know what to do when monitoring itself is delegated to automated systems that the nominal monitor cannot meaningfully oversee. The entity that designed the automated system is contractually insulated. The individual nominally responsible faces criminal exposure only for concealment, not for structurally foreclosed judgment. And the doctrine that would not require individual fault — products liability — does not yet reach software in the United States.

## Part III. The Common Structure

### 5. Five Fracture Points

The three domains examined in Part II differ in subject matter, governing doctrine, and institutional context. A fair question is whether this paper describes one structural problem or three analogous problems grouped under one evocative concept. The answer is closer to the latter than the former: copyright authorship, administrative due process, and enterprise cybersecurity are genuinely different bodies of law, and the doctrinal mechanisms that produce strain in each are not interchangeable. What recurs across all three is not a single doctrine but a single structural condition — a gap between what the law presumes about the human decision-maker and what the organisational architecture actually provides. The fracture points below are expressions of that condition as it manifests differently in different doctrinal contexts. Not every fracture point carries equal weight in every domain; the pattern is structurally parallel, not uniform.

#### 5.1 The notice-and-transparency fix does not resolve the control problem

Courts in *K.W. v. Armstrong*, *Houston Federation of Teachers*, and the *SyRI* decision required transparency and notice. The USCO requires disclosure of AI involvement in copyright applications. FTC consent orders require documented security programmes. These are not wrong. But they address a different problem.

Transparency addresses epistemic access — whether the affected party or the decision-maker can see how the system works. The structural problem is not epistemic but decisional: the human decision-maker lacks the authority, time, or practical capacity to act on what transparency reveals. Knowing how an algorithm calculates a benefits reduction does not give a caseworker with seven minutes per case the capacity to independently evaluate the calculation. Knowing that AI contributed to an image does not give the copyright applicant authorship over the AI-generated elements. Knowing that a security tool exists does not give a CISO the capacity to verify its configuration across a system processing millions of events daily.

The notice-and-transparency fix is a procedural response to a structural problem. It creates a record of disclosure. It does not create the conditions for judgment.

#### 5.2 The "not the determinative factor" standard is unenforceable

*Loomis* requires COMPAS not be the determinative factor in sentencing. FTC orders require designated human oversight. GDPR Article 22 prohibits "solely" automated decisions. The USCO requires that the human have "determined sufficient expressive elements" for copyright to attach. In each domain, doctrine draws a line between acceptable AI assistance and impermissible AI determination — and in each domain, the line is practically unenforceable.

These formulations create incentives for cosmetic human involvement — the precise rubber-stamping that regulators acknowledge is inadequate but no court has invalidated. A judge who reviews a COMPAS score before sentencing satisfies *Loomis*. A caseworker who clicks "approve" satisfies HITL requirements. A CISO who signs off on a security attestation satisfies compliance. In each case, the formal requirement is met. In each case, the human's practical capacity to have decided otherwise is not examined. The "not the determinative factor" standard regulates the appearance of human involvement without testing its substance.

The authorship test in creative IP and the sentencing oversight test in *Loomis* are doctrinally distinct — one requires substantive control over expression, the other requires only the formal retention of discretion. But both share the structural feature this paper examines: the human's formal role is assessed without inquiry into whether the organisational and technical conditions made the exercise of that role practically possible.

#### 5.3 Economic loss doctrine and contractual caps insulate the entity with effective control

Where contract caps and economic loss rules apply, the entity that designed and deployed the automated system may face far less exposure than the entity bearing the operational losses. The CrowdStrike litigation illustrates the pattern: Delta's $500 million in operational losses will be recovered, if at all, within contractual caps of single-digit millions, because the economic loss doctrine bars most tort claims and the court declined to treat the software as a product.

The same structure appears in creative IP. Model providers' terms of service routinely disclaim liability for infringing outputs and assign output responsibility to the user. The user who publishes bears the risk of infringement. The model developer who trained the system on copyrighted material and designed the generation process is contractually insulated from downstream claims.

In public administration, the analogous insulation operates through qualified immunity and sovereign immunity. Even where a system is found unconstitutional — as in *Cahoo* — the individual officials who configured and operationalised the system may be protected by qualified immunity, and the vendor that built it faces only contractual exposure. The result is diffused accountability: neither the state actors nor the vendor bears full responsibility for the system's constitutional failures.

A note on "effective control" is warranted. The entity that holds it is not always the same. In some cases it is the vendor who built the tool; in others the deploying organisation that configured it; in others the diffuse architecture of automation, procurement, and institutional design that no single actor controls. This diffusion is part of the problem. The law's attribution mechanisms assume that effective control is locatable — that someone, somewhere, made the relevant choice. Where control is distributed across a supply chain, a procurement process, and an automated workflow, no available attribution mechanism can locate it in a single responsible actor.

#### 5.4 Criminal liability requires intent, but the problem is structural

*Sullivan*'s conviction required active concealment. No criminal theory reaches the security professional who attests to controls they cannot meaningfully oversee. In *Cahoo*, individual-officer qualified immunity was granted because the court could not connect individual officials to specific system defects. In *Mata v. Avianca*, the attorneys were sanctioned for failing to verify AI-generated citations — but the sanction was for dishonesty in compounding the error, not for the initial reliance on the tool.

Across all three domains, criminal and quasi-criminal liability depends on locating a culpable mental state in a specific individual. The structural condition this paper describes — a human in a role that architecturally forecloses the judgment liability requires — does not produce the kind of individual fault that criminal law can recognise. The problem is not that someone acted wrongly. It is that the organisational architecture ensured that no one was positioned to act rightly.

#### 5.5 Products liability — the doctrine that does not require individual fault — does not yet reach software in the U.S.

The doctrinal irony is direct. The liability framework best suited to systems that cause harm independent of individual fault — products liability, with its strict liability for defective products, its focus on design rather than user conduct, and its capacity to reach manufacturers rather than end users — cannot be applied to the category of systems where individual fault is most structurally impossible to identify: software.

*Winter*, the Restatement (Third), *Rodgers v. Christie*, and *Delta v. CrowdStrike* maintain the majority position that standalone software is not a product. A counter-trend is emerging in consumer-harm contexts, but it has not reached the AI tools that are the focus of this paper. The EU has resolved this legislatively through the Product Liability Directive 2024/2853. The U.S. has not. Until it does, the default remains: liability flows to the user, the operator, the nominal decision-maker — because those are the entities existing doctrine can reach.

### 6. The Moral Crumple Zone as Legal Doctrine

The five fracture points identified in Section 5 are not isolated doctrinal gaps. They are expressions of a single structural condition: liability doctrine assumes a responsible subject who had the capacity to have acted otherwise, and organisational architecture systematically produces the opposite — a subject who bears formal authority without effective control.

Several scholarly traditions have named this condition. Read against the case law mapped in Part II, these frameworks offer a coherent interpretation of what the doctrinal pattern reveals: the moral crumple zone is not a design flaw in particular systems but a recurring structural feature of how legal responsibility is allocated wherever automated systems substantially determine consequential outcomes.

**The moral crumple zone.** Elish (2019) introduced the concept in the context of human-robot interaction: in complex automated systems, the human operator absorbs moral and legal responsibility when the system fails, much as an automobile crumple zone absorbs impact force, while the system's integrity is preserved. Her analysis of Air France 447, Three Mile Island, and autonomous vehicle incidents demonstrates how liability gravitates toward the human with nominal authority regardless of actual control capacity. The case law confirms the pattern: the caseworker in *Cahoo*, the teacher in *Houston Federation*, the CISO in *SolarWinds*, the end user who publishes AI-generated content — each occupies the crumple zone. They absorb blame because they are the only entity the legal system can reach.

**The responsibility gap.** Matthias (2004) identified the foundational problem: autonomous learning machines create situations where no actor can be held responsible under traditional frameworks because the manufacturer cannot predict the machine's behaviour and the operator cannot control it. Santoni de Sio and Mecacci (2021) extended this to four interconnected gaps — culpability, moral accountability, public accountability, and active responsibility — each requiring different solutions. Santoni de Sio and van den Hoven (2018) argued that liability attributions not grounded in relevant control conditions are morally unfair and practically unenforceable. The case law bears this out: *Loomis* attributes sentencing responsibility to a judge who cannot inspect the score; *K.W.* attributes review responsibility to caseworkers who cannot understand the formula; model providers attribute infringement responsibility to users who cannot verify the output.

**Technological due process.** Citron (2008) argued that automated government systems collapse individual adjudications into rulemaking while adhering to the procedural safeguards of neither. Programmers inevitably alter rules when embedding them into code, creating an unreviewable delegation of discretion. The *K.W.*, *Houston Federation*, and *Cahoo* decisions are practical illustrations of Citron's diagnosis — the algorithmic system displaced the adjudicative process the law required, and courts responded by reaching for procedural doctrines that could address the symptoms but not the structural displacement.

**Algocracy.** Danaher (2016) argued that algorithmic governance structurally constrains opportunities for human participation and comprehension. His assessment — that the complexity, opacity, and speed of algorithmic systems make meaningful human override practically impossible even when formally available — is confirmed by the cases: the judges in *Loomis* cannot interrogate COMPAS; the caseworkers in *K.W.* cannot evaluate the budget formula; the CISO in *SolarWinds* cannot independently verify every automated layer. Formal override authority exists. Practical override capacity does not.

**Enterprise liability theory.** The scholarly tradition on enterprise and vicarious liability — Kornhauser (1982), Sykes (1984), Arlen and Kraakman (1997) — argues that when individual fault is difficult to locate, enterprise liability better tracks real control than individual accountability. The cybersecurity domain's enterprise-level obligations (*Wyndham*, the OCR enforcement actions, the *Blackbaud* settlement) reflect this logic. But the logic has not been extended to the harder question: what happens when the enterprise itself cannot control the automated systems it has deployed?

**The interpretive claim.** Read together, the doctrinal survey in Part II and the fracture points in Section 5 are best understood through the moral crumple zone framework. The pattern is not a design flaw in particular systems but a recurring feature of how legal responsibility is allocated in the domains examined here — the structural consequence of applying doctrines built for a world of human decision-makers to a world of automated systems operated by humans who retain formal authority but not the conditions for its meaningful exercise.

## Part IV. Implications

### 7. The Unresolved Question

No court has held that structurally foreclosed judgment invalidates the liability otherwise assigned to the nominal decision-maker. No court has held that an organisation which structures its operations to foreclose human judgment thereby assumes the liability that would otherwise fall on the individual. The scholarship has named the problem. The cases have circled it. The question remains open.

If the gap identified in this paper is to be closed, doctrine would need to develop in at least three directions — stated here not as prescriptions but as the questions the case law has surfaced without resolving.

First, a standard that evaluates not whether a human was present but whether the human had the structural capacity to judge — information, authority, time, and intelligibility sufficient to have acted otherwise. The "not the determinative factor" formulation in *Loomis*, the "ascertainable standards" requirement in *K.W.*, and the USCO's "determined sufficient expressive elements" test all gesture toward this inquiry. None asks the question directly: could this human, in this organisational position, with these resources and this authority, have decided differently?

Second, a liability framework that can locate fault in organisational architecture — in the decision to separate knowing from deciding, to deploy opaque systems without intelligible oversight mechanisms, to structure roles so that formal authority persists while effective control migrates elsewhere. Enterprise liability doctrine in the cybersecurity context points in this direction. But it has not been generalised to the creative IP or public administration contexts, and even in cybersecurity it has not been applied to the specific condition of AI-mediated oversight.

Third, a coherent approach to software as product, at least for systems that determine consequential outcomes independent of individual human control. The EU has moved in this direction with the Product Liability Directive 2024/2853. The U.S. has not. Until the doctrine that does not require individual fault can reach the systems that make individual fault impossible to identify, the moral crumple zone will persist as the legal system's default allocation mechanism.

---

### 8. Limitations

The paper is diagnostic. It maps where existing doctrine fractures. It does not model the costs of alternative liability frameworks or propose specific legislative reforms.

The case law is moving rapidly. Several cases examined here — *Andersen v. Stability AI* (trial set Sept. 2026), the Ninth Circuit *Doe v. GitHub* interlocutory appeal, *Mobley v. Workday* — may produce merits holdings that alter the landscape described. The SolarWinds dismissal may prove to be a product of a particular enforcement environment rather than a stable doctrinal development.

The EU regulatory landscape — the Product Liability Directive, the AI Act, the Cyber Resilience Act — represents a legislative approach the paper describes but does not evaluate for effectiveness. Whether these instruments will resolve the structural problem or merely relocate it is an empirical question this paper does not address.

The paper focuses on U.S. law with comparative reference to UK, EU, and Australian authorities. Other jurisdictions may have developed doctrines not captured here.

The cybersecurity domain is the thinnest on decided cases and the most reliant on enforcement actions, regulatory guidance, and settlements. This reflects the state of the field, not a gap in the research. Cybersecurity liability law is substantially enforcement-driven, and the absence of adjudicated merits decisions is itself part of the pattern this paper documents.

Finally, this paper operates primarily at the level of doctrinal diagnosis — identifying where existing frameworks strain or fail. At points, the argument gestures toward a deeper claim about moral unfairness in current liability allocation, and toward a jurisprudential claim about the relationship between agency and legal responsibility. These are related but distinct levels of analysis. The paper is most confident at the doctrinal level. Its moral and jurisprudential implications are real but would require separate and more sustained argument to fully develop.

---

### 9. Conclusion

The law is good at extremes. It can see that no human authored a work, that no human reviewed before deprivation, that no monitoring was conducted. At the extremes, existing doctrine works.

The problem this paper has mapped is what happens between the extremes — the large and growing space in which a human is formally present in the decision chain but the organisational and technical architecture has structurally foreclosed the judgment legal responsibility presupposes. In copyright, the human publishes without the practical capacity to verify provenance. In public administration, the human reviews without the practical capacity to evaluate the system's output. In cybersecurity, the human oversees without the practical capacity to independently verify the automated layers.

Across all three domains, the doctrinal pattern is the same: the law reaches for transparency, notice, procedural safeguards, and "not the determinative factor" formulations. These are not wrong. They are incomplete. They address whether the human was present and whether the process was disclosed. They do not address whether the human could have judged.

This paper has proposed four conditions — opacity, authority deficit, capacity deficit, and intelligibility deficit — whose combination marks the point at which constrained judgment crosses into structural foreclosure. It has not claimed that these conditions are always present or that formal human oversight is always empty. It has claimed that existing doctrine lacks the tools to ask whether they are present, and therefore cannot distinguish the rubber stamp from genuine review.

Until it can, the human in the loop will remain the law's preferred point of impact — not because they are culpable, but because they are reachable.
