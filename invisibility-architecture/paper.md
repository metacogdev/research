# The Architecture of Absence: How AI Reveals the Structural Subordination of Judgment

## Abstract

The dominant narrative treats AI as a threat to human judgment in consequential domains. This paper argues that, in bureaucratic and compliance-heavy settings, the framing is backwards. Organisational architectures have long separated contextual knowledge from decision-making authority, replacing discretion with compliance and designing roles around narrow output formats. AI does not displace human judgment. It reveals that judgment had already been subordinated to functional power — overruled, displaced upward, or rendered non-operative by design. When AI automates rule application, classification, and consistency checking, it exposes how little decisive force human judgment had retained within the workflow. The paper traces this subordination through deskilling, the separation of knowing from deciding, the AI exposure effect, and the moral crumple zone created when human-in-the-loop governance assigns responsibility without authority. The paper is diagnostic, not prescriptive. Its question is not how to keep humans in the loop, but whether organisations are willing to let human judgment govern anything once they get there.

**Keywords:** AI governance, human judgment, deskilling, organisational architecture, human-in-the-loop, moral crumple zone, authority, discretion

## 1. Introduction: The Forcing Function

A system arrives that can perform judgment-shaped work: apply rules, match patterns, classify within parameters, and produce outputs in the expected format, on time, at scale. The organisation must confront a question it has been avoiding: what was the human actually contributing?

If the human in the role was exercising genuine judgment — interpreting purpose, weighing competing considerations under irreducible uncertainty, accepting responsibility for a decision that could not be reduced to rule application — then AI poses a genuine displacement threat. But if the human was executing a closed prompt — following a procedure, applying a rubric, producing the expected output shape without engaging with purpose — then the displacement narrative is wrong. In many organisational settings, the issue is not that judgment disappeared as a human capacity. It is that judgment was present without decisive force: the role could register, voice, or document expert interpretation while operative power resided elsewhere.

This second condition is widespread across bureaucratic, managerial, and professional settings where consequential decisions are organised through workflows, protocols, and hierarchies — including welfare administration, public employment services, criminal justice, healthcare, insurance adjudication, and other compliance-heavy domains. That prevalence is not accidental. It reflects decades of choices in work design, authority allocation, process standardisation, and institutional governance that narrowed the conditions for judgment while relocating operative power elsewhere in the system. AI does not create this condition. It reveals it by automating the compliance-shaped activity that had concealed the prior subordination of judgment to functional power.

The argument proceeds as follows. Section 2 traces the historical process by which organisations have deskilled work, extending the Braverman thesis from industrial labour into contemporary knowledge and professional roles. Section 3 identifies the specific architectural mechanism — the separation of contextual knowledge from decision-making authority — that subordinates judgment to other forms of operative power in the organisational settings this paper examines. Section 4 introduces the AI exposure effect: the claim that AI makes this prior subordination visible by automating the mechanical throughput that was masking it. Section 5 examines the governance response — human-in-the-loop oversight — and argues that it inherits and formalises the pre-existing authority split, creating what Elish (2019) calls a moral crumple zone. Section 6 offers a heuristic contrast from cybernetics and distributed cognition, naming the architectural property — co-location of sensing and responding — that the settings described in Sections 2-5 have demoted or displaced. Section 7 raises the possibility that prolonged operation under compliance regimes degrades the capacity for judgment itself. Section 8 draws implications for governance without prescribing solutions. Section 9 addresses limitations, and Section 10 concludes.

Four terms recur throughout the paper and should be distinguished at the outset. *Judgment* refers to the integrated act of interpreting a situation in light of purpose, weighing considerations under irreducible uncertainty, and accepting responsibility for the resulting decision. *Discretion* is the formally or informally recognised space within which judgment may operate — the scope of permissible interpretation. *Authority* is the institutional right to act on one's judgment — to decide, not merely to recommend. *Contextual knowledge* is the situated understanding of the particular case that arises from proximity to it. The paper's central claim is that organisational architectures have separated these four elements — distributing them across different roles, levels, and processes — so that no single position holds all four simultaneously.

The paper is diagnostic. It does not propose a new organisational model or offer design recommendations. It identifies a structural condition — the separation of knowing from deciding, and the resulting subordination of judgment to functional power — that AI makes hard to ignore.

## 2. Deskilling as Structural Policy

In the bureaucratic and compliance-heavy settings this paper examines, the narrowing of judgment is not an accident or a side effect. It is a recurring organising logic.

### From craft to factory

Braverman (1974) provides the foundational account. The transition from craft production to industrial manufacture was not simply a shift in tools or scale. It was a systematic reorganisation of the labour process around a single principle: the separation of conception from execution. Under craft production, the worker held both the plan and the skill. Under industrial management, planning was relocated to management, and the worker retained only the physical execution of tasks specified by others. This was not an optimisation. It was a transfer of control.

Braverman's thesis has been extensively critiqued — for underestimating worker resistance, for romanticising pre-industrial craft, for treating deskilling as a unidirectional process (Wood, 1982). These critiques are important. They do not, however, refute the structural observation. The critiques establish that deskilling is contested, uneven, and mediated by institutional context. They do not establish that it has not occurred.

### From factory to office

The same logic that decomposed craft into factory tasks has been extended into knowledge and professional work. The mechanisms differ — you cannot bolt a clinical decision to an assembly line — but the structural principle is the same: translate judgment into procedure, standardise the procedure, and compress the role to the execution of the procedure.

Clinical guidelines, sentencing matrices, benefits eligibility rulesets, credit scoring models, insurance adjudication protocols, contributor guidelines for open-source projects — these are all instances of the same architectural move. Contextual judgment is translated into a decision tree. The decision tree is standardised. The worker's role becomes the execution of the tree. Judgment about whether the tree is appropriate, whether the case before them falls in the gap between the rule's language and its intent, is not part of the instruction.

Timmermans and Berg (1997, 2003) document this process in medicine with particular precision. Their analysis of medical protocols shows that standardisation does not simply replace situated judgment. Protocols become workable through local adaptation — clinicians interpret, adjust, and renegotiate the protocol in practice. But the adaptation is constrained. Protocols narrow what counts as legitimate judgment by defining the range of acceptable responses and making departures from the standard accountable in ways that conformity is not. The result is not the elimination of all discretion but its relocation: from the substantive question (what should be done for this patient?) to the procedural question (can this departure from protocol be justified?).

Adler and Borys (1996) complicate any one-way deskilling story by distinguishing enabling bureaucracy from coercive bureaucracy. Standardisation can scaffold discretion — providing resources, checklists, and reference points that help workers exercise judgment more effectively. Or it can suppress discretion — mandating compliance with procedures whose rationale is opaque and whose violation triggers sanction. The distinction is real and important. But it is a distinction in design intent, not a refutation of the structural trend. The question is not whether enabling bureaucracy is possible. It is which version most organisations have built. This paper's claim is not that all standardisation suppresses judgment — in some domains, procedural standardisation protects fairness and prevents arbitrary exercises of power. The claim is that in the bureaucratic, managerial, and professional settings where AI is being deployed into consequential workflows — public administration, healthcare, criminal justice, insurance, and other compliance-heavy domains — the dominant form has been coercive: standardisation designed to compress the role, not to scaffold the judgment within it.

Audit and metric regimes reinforce this tendency by shifting decision weight toward what can be standardised, documented, compared, and defended in review (Power, 1997; Strathern, 2000; Espeland and Sauder, 2007). Workers may still know what a case requires, but outcomes are increasingly governed by indicators, targets, rankings, and auditable procedure. Judgment remains present, but it no longer reliably governs. Expertise is retained inside the workflow while decisional force is relocated elsewhere.

### The street-level condition

Lipsky (1980/2010) provides the answer from the public sector. Street-level bureaucrats — caseworkers, police officers, teachers, social workers — retain formal discretion. Their roles are defined in terms that presuppose judgment: assess needs, determine eligibility, exercise professional judgment. But the conditions under which they work — chronic scarcity, overwhelming caseloads, ambiguous mandates, and institutional pressure for consistent outputs — push them systematically toward routines, simplifications, and mass processing. Lipsky's account is not about individual failure. It is about a structural environment that makes genuine judgment practically impossible even when it is formally required.

The gap between formal discretion and practical capacity is the central condition this paper examines. The organisation says the role involves judgment. The architecture of the role ensures that judgment can rarely be exercised as the role formally requires.

### Prompts for humans

Sambasivan and Veeraraghavan (2022) extend this analysis into AI development itself, showing that domain experts are reduced to data collectors and labellers within AI pipelines — their expertise invisibilised inside the infrastructure that transforms knowledge into training data. The expert's contextual understanding is needed to produce the data but not to shape the decisions made with it.

This observation generalises. Many roles in the protocolised settings this paper examines are structured as constrained imperatives with narrow output formats. Apply this rubric. Complete this form. Score against these criteria. Escalate if the score exceeds this threshold. The worker learns to produce the expected output shape without engaging with purpose, because purpose is not part of the instruction. The instruction specifies what to produce, not why. The role is, functionally, a prompt: a constrained directive with an expected output format, executed by a human who has been trained to comply rather than to judge.

This is not a failure of individual effort. It is a design property of the role. And it predates AI by decades.

## 3. The Authority Split: Knowing Without Deciding, Deciding Without Knowing

If deskilling removes the substance of judgment from the task, the authority split removes its operative force from the structure. Even in roles where the task retains interpretive demands, the organisational architecture ensures that the person who knows cannot decide, and the person who decides does not know.

### Decision premises and bounded rationality

Simon (1947/1997) established the foundational framework. Organisations manage bounded rationality by structuring decision premises — the assumptions, values, and factual inputs within which decisions are made. Authority in Simon's framework is not simply the right to command. It is the capacity to define the premises within which others decide. Some actors set the frame; others decide within it. This is not a pathology. It is the basic mechanism by which organisations coordinate behaviour across individuals with different knowledge and different positions.

But the mechanism has a structural consequence that Simon himself recognised as a design trade-off. The person who sets the premises is removed from the context in which the decision operates. The person who operates in the context is bounded by premises they did not set. Both are doing their jobs. Simon's framework does not claim that judgment becomes impossible — premise-bounded actors still decide within their scope. But the scope itself is the constraint. When the premises are narrow, the procedures specific, and the acceptable output formats pre-defined, the decision within premises collapses toward execution. The gap between bounded judgment and structured compliance is a matter of degree, and the organisational trend documented throughout this paper has been to narrow that gap until the decision within premises is indistinguishable from the execution of a rule.

### The machine bureaucracy

Mintzberg (1979/1980) gives this structural separation a specific organisational form. In the machine bureaucracy, the operating core — the people who do the work — has minimal discretion. The technostructure — analysts, planners, process designers — defines the standards, procedures, and rules that govern the operating core's behaviour. Decision-making authority resides in the strategic apex. The operating core executes; it does not decide. The technostructure designs; it does not confront.

Mintzberg's taxonomy is not normative. He identifies conditions under which machine bureaucracy is effective: simple, stable environments with standardisable tasks. The point is that this form has been extended far beyond those conditions — into professional services, public administration, knowledge work, healthcare — precisely because it offers the coordination benefits of predictability and control, at the cost of the judgment it claims to preserve.

### Formal authority versus real authority

Aghion and Tirole (1997) make the separation between knowledge and authority explicit. They distinguish formal authority — the legal or organisational right to decide — from real authority — effective control grounded in possessing the relevant information. The two can diverge systematically. A frontline worker may have real authority in the sense that they possess the information necessary to make a good decision, but lack formal authority to act on it. A senior executive may hold formal authority but lack the contextual information to exercise it meaningfully.

Dessein (2002) shows that this separation is often intentional. Centralising authority creates communication and adaptation costs, but it preserves control. Delegating authority to the person with knowledge improves adaptation but reduces predictability. Organisations choose the separation of knowing from deciding because the alternative — letting the person who knows also decide — means tolerating decisions that management cannot predict or control.

### Hierarchy as knowledge-economising structure

Garicano (2000) models hierarchy as a structure for economising on knowledge. Routine problems are handled at lower levels; exceptions are escalated upward. The system is efficient: it minimises the knowledge each actor needs by restricting their decision scope. But it means that the person confronting the case — the caseworker, the clinician, the front-line officer — handles only those aspects of the case that fall within their pre-authorised decision scope. Everything else is escalated to someone who has the authority but not the context.

The escalation itself transforms the information. What reaches the decision-maker is not the case as experienced by the frontline worker but a summary, a classification, a score — information processed into a format compatible with the decision-maker's scope. The contextual richness that would support judgment is filtered out by the same process that routes the case to authority.

### The judgment blockage

Maynard-Moody and Musheno (2003) document the human cost of this structure. Their study of frontline public servants — cops, teachers, counsellors — reveals a persistent tension between the state-agent identity (follow the rules, apply the policy) and the citizen-agent identity (respond to the person in front of you, exercise judgment about what this situation requires). Workers are forced to choose. The structure does not allow both.

This is what might be called a judgment blockage — not inactivity, since everyone is busy, but a structural condition in which the components necessary for integrated judgment are distributed across positions that cannot combine them. The frontline worker has the contextual knowledge but not the authority to act on it. The decision-maker has the authority but not the contextual knowledge. Process fills the gap between them. Memos flow upward. Policies flow downward. Cases are processed. Decisions are made. Expert judgment may still be present at the point of contact, but it does not govern the outcome, because the architecture has distributed its components to different locations and vested operative force elsewhere.

This is not inefficiency. It is an intentional design choice that trades judgment for predictability and control. It is the architecture into which AI is being deployed.

## 4. The AI Exposure Effect

AI does not create the subordination of judgment. It makes it visible.

### Technology as occasion for structuring

Barley (1986) showed that technology does not determine organisational outcomes. The same CT scanners produced different effects in two radiology departments because existing institutional arrangements shaped how they were incorporated. Technology is an occasion for structuring: it reveals and rearranges existing configurations rather than imposing new ones.

The same principle applies to AI. When an AI system enters an organisational workflow, it does not encounter a blank slate. It enters a structure that has already allocated authority, defined decision scopes, protocolised procedures, and distributed knowledge. What the AI does within that structure depends on the structure, not only on the AI.

### The regime of knowing

Pachidi et al. (2021) show that algorithm introduction changes the regime of knowing — what counts as valid knowledge, whose judgment matters, and what kinds of action are legitimate. In their study of a financial services firm, algorithmic tools did not simply add a new information source. They restructured the knowledge hierarchy: whose interpretations carried weight, what evidence counted, and how decisions were justified. The shift was not from human judgment to machine judgment, but from one regime of institutional knowing to another.

This is the AI exposure effect. AI automates the routinised component of work — the rule application, classification, and pattern matching that made the role visibly busy. Where that routinised component had become the authoritative layer of the role — where expert interpretation could be present but non-binding under the conditions described in Sections 2 and 3 — automation does not so much displace judgment as reveal how little operative force it retained. The substitution is diagnostic: AI can replace the visible work because the decisive work had already been reorganised into structured execution, escalation, and control. As the regime of knowing shifts, previously tacit assumptions about who knows, who decides, and whose interpretation governs become legible.

### Engagement, interrogation, and default

Lebovitz, Lifshitz-Assaf, and Levina (2022) provide micro-level evidence. In AI-assisted medical diagnosis, augmentation was not automatic. AI improved judgment only where physicians developed interrogation practices — strategies for questioning, contextualising, and challenging its outputs. Without those practices, AI was either ignored or blindly accepted.

The critical finding is institutional, not individual. Time pressure, accountability structures, training, and professional norms shaped whether interrogation practices developed. Where those conditions were absent, the human role collapsed to rubber-stamping. AI did not cause the collapse. It made an already impractical form of engagement visible.

### Institutional mediation

Christin (2017) reinforces the point by comparing web journalism and criminal justice. In both settings, algorithmic metrics entered professional workflows, but their effects on judgment diverged. Journalists retained selective engagement, integrating metrics alongside editorial norms. In criminal justice, a different institutional logic produced a different pattern. The point is not that one setting is better. It is that institutional context, not technology alone, determines how algorithmic tools reshape the conditions for judgment.

The implication is that AI has no single effect on human judgment. It has the effect the institutional architecture permits. Where practitioners had authority, context, and norms that valued interpretation, AI could augment. Where judgment had already been subordinated — where practitioners could interpret but not govern, or were reduced to executing closed prompts within narrow output formats — AI exposed that subordination by performing the compliant version of the role as well as or better than the human.

This diagnosis does not apply uniformly. In some settings, proceduralisation protects fairness — sentencing guidelines can constrain judicial arbitrariness; eligibility rules can prevent caseworker discrimination — and AI can genuinely support rather than displace professional judgment. The paper's claim is not that AI always reveals judgment as absent. It is that in the compliance-heavy settings examined here, it more often reveals a familiar pattern: expert interpretation persists, but operative power lies elsewhere.

### From street-level to system-level

Bullock (2019) and Bovens and Zouridis (2002) trace the structural trajectory. Bovens and Zouridis argue that information and communication technology shifts discretion from frontline officials to system designers: the bureaucracy moves from street-level to system-level, and the human at the front line no longer has meaningful control over the decisions processed through them. Bullock connects this trajectory to AI, arguing that artificial intelligence in bureaucracy should be understood in relation to discretion and administrative structure, not merely as a technical tool.

Kellogg, Valentine, and Christin (2020) synthesise the broader evidence: algorithms intensify monitoring, evaluation, and standardisation while also provoking contestation and adaptation. They are, in the authors' phrase, the new contested terrain of control. But the terrain was already contested. Algorithms did not invent the conflict between institutional control and worker discretion. They sharpened it, by making the control more precise, the monitoring more comprehensive, and the space for discretion more visibly constrained.

### What is left?

When AI handles the mechanical throughput — rule application, classification, pattern matching, consistency checking — what is left for the human? If the organisation has maintained the conditions for genuine judgment — contextual knowledge, interpretive authority, accountability for the outcome — then what remains is the most valuable part of the work: the interpretation that only a situated, responsible agent can provide.

But if the organisation has already arranged those conditions so that the role's interpretation does not decide — if the role was structured as a closed prompt, if the authority to interpret had migrated to the technostructure, if the contextual knowledge was being filtered out by the same processes that routed decisions to authority — then what remains for the human is exposure. Not because AI took meaningful judgment away. Because AI reveals that the meaningful judgment in the workflow was already non-operative, advisory, or bypassed. It strips away the activity that had been concealing the deadlock.

## 5. The Crumple Zone: Responsibility Without Authority

The governance response to AI in consequential domains is human-in-the-loop oversight. In settings where the human retains genuine interpretive authority, contextual knowledge, and time to engage — some clinical settings, some judicial contexts, some professional partnerships — HITL can function as intended. But in the bureaucratic and compliance-heavy settings that are the focus of this paper, HITL assumes that a human in the loop has the contextual knowledge and the decision-making authority to exercise judgment. The organisational architecture has separated these. The human may retain expertise. What the human lacks is the authority for that expertise to govern the outcome. They have responsibility.

### The moral crumple zone

Elish (2019) introduces the concept of the moral crumple zone: the structural position in which a human absorbs blame when an automated system fails, despite having minimal ability to intervene in the system's operation. The term is drawn from automotive engineering — crumple zones are designed to deform on impact, absorbing the force that would otherwise reach the passenger. In human-machine systems, the human serves the same function: they absorb the moral and legal liability that the system cannot.

Elish's analysis is not about bad actors or poorly designed interfaces. It is about a structural position that human-machine systems routinely create. The human is placed in the system because governance requires human involvement. The system is designed so that the human's involvement is structurally limited. When the system fails, the human bears responsibility for an outcome they could not meaningfully have influenced.

### The insertion fallacy

Crootof, Kaminski, and Price (2022) identify a related failure: the tendency to insert humans into automated processes to "address algorithmic incapacities" without understanding the resulting sociotechnical system. The logic is: the algorithm cannot do X, so put a human in to do X. But the human is inserted into a workflow designed around the algorithm, under time pressures defined by the algorithm's throughput, with information presented in the algorithm's format, and with authority limited to accepting or rejecting the algorithm's output. The human is not doing X. They are performing the appearance of doing X within constraints that make X impossible.

This is not a design oversight that better interfaces could solve. It is a structural consequence of inserting a human into an architecture designed for automation. The architecture shapes the human's role, not the reverse.

### Quasi-automation

Wagner (2019) names the condition directly: quasi-automation, in which humans are nominally involved in automated decision processes but function as rubber stamps. The liability remains with the human. The decision-making has migrated to the system. Wagner's contribution is to identify this as a structural condition, not a behavioural one. The human is not lazy or inattentive. They are operating within a system that has been designed so that meaningful intervention is structurally impractical.

Chiodo et al. (2025) formalise this through computability theory, distinguishing HITL setup types by how much the system's output actually depends on the human. In what they call "trivial monitoring," the system defines a total function independent of the human — the human can halt the process but cannot alter its computation. The human is present. The system's output is unaffected by what the human thinks. The HITL requirement is satisfied. Nothing has been governed.

### Embedded policy constraints

Allhutter et al. (2020) provide a concrete case. The Austrian Public Employment Service introduced an algorithmic profiling system (AMS algorithm) that classified job seekers into categories determining the level of support they received. The system embedded policy priorities — austerity constraints, efficiency targets, resource allocation logic — into the workflow itself. By the time a caseworker encountered a client, the algorithmic classification had already constrained what the caseworker could do. Discretion had not been removed by the caseworker's choice. It had been architecturally foreclosed before the caseworker acted.

Sztandar-Sztanderska (2025) generalises this finding. Her analysis of human oversight in welfare administration shows that oversight quality is shaped by policy conditions, organisational conditions, professional conditions, and technological conditions — not merely by the cognitive capabilities of the human in the loop. The failure of oversight is not a failure of attention or competence. It is a failure of the conditions under which oversight occurs.

### The governance assumption

Green (2022) states the implication directly: policies requiring human oversight of government algorithms routinely fail to produce meaningful oversight in practice. The failure modes — time pressure, institutional incentives to defer, information asymmetry between the human and the system, lack of training in how to interrogate algorithmic outputs — are not aberrations. They are the predictable consequences of inserting a human oversight requirement into an organisational architecture that has already migrated decision-making authority elsewhere.

Alston (2019) extends this observation to digital welfare states globally, arguing that digitisation and automation intensify opacity and administrative distancing even when human officials remain formally present. The human is in the building. The human may even be in the loop. But the loop has been designed so that the human's presence satisfies a governance requirement without constituting governance.

The regulatory conclusion follows from the structural diagnosis: the regulatory unit should be the human-in-the-loop system, not the human alone (see Crootof, Kaminski, and Price, 2022, who develop this argument in the context of algorithmic governance). Regulating the human — requiring their presence, mandating their training, auditing their decisions — cannot produce meaningful governance if the system within which they operate has been designed so that their judgment does not govern the outcome.

### HITL inherits the split

The moral crumple zone is not a new pathology created by AI. It is the formalisation of the pre-existing authority split identified in Section 3. The person closest to the case has always had the richest understanding and the least authority. HITL governance does not solve this problem. It names the person who will be blamed when it fails.

## 6. Co-location as Heuristic Contrast

The argument so far has identified a structural separation — contextual knowledge from decision-making authority — and shown that AI exposes it. But the separation implies a referent: what would the non-separated version look like? Two traditions offer a heuristic contrast — not a model for organisational design, but a way to name the property that these settings have demoted, displaced, or split apart.

Beer's (1972/1984) Viable System Model identifies a specific architectural property that viable systems require: recursive local autonomy. Each level of the system must contain the regulatory capacity to sense its environment, respond to it, and coordinate with other units, without requiring centralised permission for every adaptive response. When local units lose this capacity, the system becomes dependent on centralised intervention — which is slower, less contextual, and less responsive. The system does not fail immediately. It becomes brittle: capable of handling expected conditions but incapable of adapting to novelty.

Beer also identifies a specific escalation mechanism — the algedonic alert — that differs structurally from the escalation described in Section 3. In the bureaucratic model, escalation is triggered by scope boundaries: the case exceeds the frontline worker's authorised decision range. In Beer's model, escalation is triggered by a gap between local regulatory capacity and environmental demand. The gap, not the hierarchy, determines when escalation occurs. The frontline unit retains as much autonomy as its capacity supports.

Hutchins (1995) provides complementary evidence from a different tradition: his study of navigation teams shows that system-level cognitive performance depends on the organisation of the whole activity system — who communicates with whom, what information flows through which channels, who has authority to act on what. Change the communication structure and you change the cognitive properties of the system. This is not a biological analogy. It is an empirical finding about how distributed human work actually functions when sensing and responding are co-located within the team.

The relevance to this paper is heuristic, not prescriptive. Beer and Hutchins do not supply a model for organisational redesign — organisations face constraints (legal accountability, democratic legitimacy, coordination at scale) that neither cybernetic nor distributed-cognition frameworks fully address. What they supply is a name for the property that Sections 2-5 describe as subordinated: the capacity of local units to sense and respond at the point of contact. The contrast sharpens the diagnosis without extending the paper's claims beyond it.

## 7. A Speculative Extension: From Subordination to Incapacity

The argument so far has been structural: in the settings this paper examines, organisations subordinate judgment by separating knowing from deciding. A further possibility, less well established empirically, deserves brief consideration: that prolonged operation under compliance regimes may not merely subordinate judgment but degrade the capacity for it.

Bainbridge (1983) identified this dynamic in the context of industrial automation. Automating routine tasks removes the practice opportunities that operators need to develop and maintain the skills for handling exceptions. The more reliable the automation, the less the human practises. The less the human practises, the less competent they are when the automation fails and they must intervene. The irony is structural: the system removes the conditions under which competence is maintained.

The parallel to organisational judgment is suggestive. If the conditions for developing judgment are practice under genuine uncertainty, authority to act on interpretation, and feedback from consequences, then a compliance regime removes all three. The caseworker who spends years applying rules without interpreting purpose may not retain a dormant judgment capacity waiting to be activated.

This is a structural inference, not an empirically settled finding. Longitudinal studies measuring judgment degradation under compliance regimes do not yet exist at the scale required to settle the question. But the inference matters for governance: if the atrophy thesis is even partly correct, then reconstituting judgment requires more than a structural rearrangement. It may require something closer to institutional rehabilitation — rebuilding skills, norms, and authority structures that have not merely been set aside but have weakened from disuse.

## 8. Implications for Governance

This paper does not propose solutions. It diagnoses a condition. If that diagnosis is right, what follows for governance frameworks that require human judgment in consequential operations?

### The institutional preconditions for judgment

Any protocol requiring human judgment must reckon with the fact that the institutional preconditions for judgment to govern outcomes have been systematically reorganised away from the frontline. Frameworks that demand a human who can set constraints, interpret purpose, and accept liability are designing for a figure the institution has spent decades selecting against and training out.

The institutional landscape does not currently produce such a figure. Producing one requires reversing the architectural choices described above — giving the human both the contextual knowledge and the authority to act on it at the point where the decision operates.

### The unavoidable question

The question “do you actually want judgment here?” is now unavoidable. As long as a human occupied the seat, organisations could treat human presence as evidence that judgment was present.

Once a system can occupy that seat and perform the compliant version better — faster, more consistently, at lower cost — the pretence collapses. In many of these settings, the human was not exercising governing judgment but executing a constrained procedure, and the system can execute that procedure more reliably.

This leaves two analytic poles. Restructure the role so that judgment is possible — restore contextual knowledge, interpretive authority, and accountability to the position. Or admit that the role was never really about judgment, and that the governance claim of “human judgment” was nominal. Most organisations will occupy positions between these poles, restoring judgment in some workflows while retaining proceduralisation in others. The contrast clarifies the question each role must answer: is the human here to judge, or to comply?

Both directions are politically uncomfortable. Restoring judgment means tolerating slower throughput, inconsistent outputs, and decisions management cannot fully predict. Admitting its subordination means admitting that decades of governance language about human judgment governing outcomes was, in material terms, nominal.

### Not a training problem

Reconstituting judgment is not a training problem. Training humans to exercise judgment within an architecture that blocks it produces frustration, not governance. Nor is it a technology problem. Better oversight interfaces cannot compensate for the absence of the authority and contextual knowledge that judgment requires.

It is an authority problem. It requires collapsing the separation of knowing from deciding by giving the person closest to the case both the information and the authority to act on it. That, in turn, means tolerating the consequences of genuine judgment: slower throughput, inconsistent outputs, local adaptation, and decisions that cannot be predicted from procedure alone.

This is the structural reversal the diagnosis requires. It is also what organisational architectures in these settings have been designed to prevent.

## 9. Limitations and Open Questions

### Scope

The paper is diagnostic. It does not model the costs of reconstituting judgment or propose an organisational design that resolves the authority split. These are important questions. They are not this paper's questions.

### Empirical grounding

The atrophy thesis (Section 7) is a structural inference, not an empirically demonstrated finding. It is presented as a speculative extension of the paper's core argument, not as a settled claim.

### Standardisation and fairness

The paper does not distinguish sharply enough between settings where standardisation primarily subordinates judgment and settings where it protects fairness. Sentencing guidelines, benefits rules, and clinical protocols were often introduced to constrain arbitrary or discriminatory discretion. The relationship between proceduralisation and justice is not one-directional: standardisation can be fairness-enhancing and judgment-thinning at the same time. The diagnosis applies most clearly where proceduralisation has crossed from constraining arbitrariness to making expert interpretation non-governing in practice. Identifying that boundary more precisely is a task this paper does not undertake.

### Sector scope

The paper draws primarily from public administration, healthcare, criminal justice, and adjacent professional bureaucracy contexts, but the architecture it identifies is not unique to the public sector. Similar separations of expertise from operative authority appear wherever workflows, managerial hierarchies, compliance systems, or resource logics overrule the judgment of those closest to the case. That is why the paper's object is architectural rather than sectoral. The diagnosis may still apply differently in organisational forms where authority and context are less rigidly separated: startups, cooperatives, professional partnerships, research laboratories, craft workshops. How far it generalises across organisational types remains an empirical question.

### Open questions

Three questions warrant further investigation:

First, is the moral crumple zone a design flaw or a structural necessity of scaled automated systems? Any system operating at sufficient scale and speed may produce positions in which humans bear responsibility for outcomes they cannot meaningfully influence. If so, the question is not how to eliminate the crumple zone but how to govern it honestly.

Second, at what point does judgment subordination become judgment atrophy, and is the transition reversible? The distinction matters for governance: if the capacity is present but structurally non-operative, reform of authority relations may suffice. If it has atrophied, rehabilitation is required. The answer likely varies by context, duration, and individual, but a structural account of the transition is needed.

Third, can organisations reconstitute the authority conditions for judgment without abandoning the coordination benefits of hierarchy? Whether recursive local autonomy, as described in Beer’s (1984) viable system model, is feasible under real legal, political, and economic constraints remains open.

---

## 10. Conclusion: The Diagnostic Mirror

In the bureaucratic and compliance-heavy settings examined here, AI functions as a diagnostic instrument. It does not create the subordination of human judgment. It reveals that judgment had already been structurally overruled, displaced, or rendered non-operative by decades of choices that separated knowing from deciding, replaced discretion with compliance, and designed roles as constrained imperatives with narrow output formats.

The exposure is simple. When a system can perform the compliant version of a role — rule application, pattern matching, classification within parameters — the organisation must confront whether the human in that role was doing something the system cannot. If the answer is yes, the human contribution is clarified and can be protected. If the answer is no, the role had already been organised so that compliance work governed the outcome more than expert interpretation did, and the displacement narrative misdescribes a condition that preceded the technology.

The governance question, properly framed, is not how to keep humans in the loop. It is whether organisations are willing to reconstitute the conditions under which human judgment in the loop would have binding force. The question recurs across sectors whenever expertise is formally retained while effective power is organised elsewhere.

More specifically: deskilling and protocolisation have thinned the interpretive substance of roles (Section 2); the separation of contextual knowledge from decision-making authority has subordinated expert judgment to other forms of operative power (Section 3); AI automates the compliance-shaped activity that concealed this condition, making the subordination visible (Section 4); and HITL governance inherits the authority split rather than correcting it, producing responsibility without power (Section 5). Taken together, these findings suggest that the central challenge for AI governance in these settings is not technological but institutional: whether the person closest to the case will be given both the contextual knowledge and the authority to make judgment govern the outcome.

This paper does not prescribe how that restructuring should proceed. It identifies the conditions that make it necessary.
